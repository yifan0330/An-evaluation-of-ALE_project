The findings in table \ref{table: emotion} summarize the number of voxels in ALE difference maps
(corresponding to every combination of two emotions) with p-value $<0.05$.
\begin{itemize}
\item The diagonal entries $0$ indicate that the voxelwise ALE difference would be like a null
    if same type of emotion is used for ALE subtraction analysis
\item Surprise and anger emotions show the largest similarity with only $694$ voxels
    significantly different, while disgust and happy emotions are the least similar combinations.
    And the differences are illustrated in figure \ref{fig:surprise & anger} and figure
    \ref{fig:disgust & happy} intuitively.
\end{itemize}
\begin{figure}
\centering
\begin{subfigure}{.5\textwidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{surprise_anger.png}
\caption{ALE difference map with emotions: surprise \& anger}
\label{fig:surprise & anger}
\end{subfigure}%
\begin{subfigure}{.5\textwidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{disgust_happy.png}
\caption{ALE difference map with emotions: disgust \& happy}
\label{fig:disgust & happy}
\end{subfigure}
\end{figure}
However, although the coordinates with significant differences in ALE values regarding any two
emotions are easily computed, the specific regions where they appear haven't be investigated in this
report.

An Evaluation of ALE Method in Coordinate-based Meta-analysisYifan Yuyifan.yu@keble.ox.ac.ukSupervisor: Prof. Thomas NicholsAugust 28, 2020AbstractTo overcome the common drawbacks of small sample size and high prevalence of false posi-tives in fMRI studies, meta-analysis is usually conducted for detecting consistent activation regionsassociated with a specific cognitive task across studies. Coordinate-based meta-analysis (CBMA)constitute the most common approach for neuroimaging meta-analysis, and Activation likelihood es-timation (ALE) is a representative approach in CBMA. This report describes an evaluation of ALEmethods in terms of statistical properties in finding activation regions and robustness on inclusion ofnoise studies, and its extension to two-sample comparison is also investigated. It can be concludedthat the power properties of ALE depends on the number of valid studies, regardless of the numberof total studies, but the number of total studies would influence the permutated ALE difference map.Additionally, ALE subtraction method is applied to a real dataset of emotion studies for verification.1 IntroductionFunctional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) has become a dominant technique in neuroimagingresearch since its development in the early 1990s, as it images brain activity in a non-invasive waywith high spatial resolution and relatively high temporal resolution [7]. The increasing literature infMRI is followed by a growing need to integrate findings, establish consistency and heterogeneity acrossindependent but similar studies. Additionally, the validity of fMRI studies is challenged by commondrawbacks of small sample size, high prevalence of false positives (roughly 10−20% of reported activa-tions in published works [14]), significant heterogeneity among studies and unreliable inference due totheir diversity in measurements and types of analysis [11]. But meta-analyses is significantly helpful inaddressing these limitations and improving statistical power by pooling evidence from multiple studies,and ultimately provide a reasonably good estimate to the true effect size and distinguish from thoseresults happened by chance.Meta-analysis in neuroimaging research is generally categorized into two approaches:image-based meta-analysis (IBMA)which provides full statistic maps andcoordinate-based meta-analysis (CBMA)whichonly reports activation foci in a standard space. Although information loss due to relatively sparserepresentation of image results, as well as sensitivity of foci coordinates caused by research procedure(eg, the number of foci per cluster to report), are two major limitations ofCBMA[8],CBMAis stillthe main approach for the meta-analysis of fMRI studies, since it is normally impossible to accessentire statistical map of published studies [11]. AndCBMAis the chosen meta-analysis method inthis report, with data in the form ofx,y,zbrain atlas coordinates representing the local maxima inactivation regions.Motivated by the aim to identify the regions in human brain showing consistent activation acrossstudies,activation likelihood estimation (ALE),multilevel kernel density analysis (MKDA)andsigneddifferential mapping (SDM)are commonly used as kernel-based meta-analysis methods [11]. Thesekernel-based methods share similar procedure for generating statistical maps: it starts with constructingstatistic maps by convolving three-dimensional spatial kernels centered at each reported foci, then theyare combined to create several study-specific statistic maps, ultimately they are summarized into asingle statistic map to represent consistent activation across all studies involved. Significance of thestatistic map is tested with reference to a Monte Carlo distribution under the null hypothesis that1
there is no consistent activation across studies [10]. However, there are several shortcomings of kernel-based methods, for example, the width of spatial kernels is constant throughout the brain, they are notbased on statistical models and lack interpretability, and only voxelwise p-values are computed [4, 3].Consequently, several Bayesian model-based methods have been proposed and solved these drawbacks,which are categorized into parametric spatial point process models [3, 15] and non-parametric Bayesianmodels [4]. Although they generally provide advances in interpretability and accuracy over kernel-basedmethods, they also exhibit several drawbacks, for example, they are limited to one-sample setting,mostly are not generative or predictive models, and some approaches cannot conduct meta-regressionsince study level covariates are not considered [11, 4].But the discussion is basically limited to performance of kernel-based methods in this report, especiallyreferring to ALE method.
